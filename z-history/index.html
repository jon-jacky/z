Z History

Jon Jacky

Annotated links to sources (by others, not me) that document significant moments in the history of the Z notation. They trace the growth, then decline, of interest in Z.

More pages about Z.

Approaches to proof in Z by Andrew Martin (1997): "Various attempts at supporting proof in Z are described in the literature. This paper presents a survey of these approaches, and the underlying semantic issues which make proof in Z a non-trivial task. ... Special care is given to an account of the peculiarities of Z schemas. ... The broad conclusion is that none of these approaches is a clear winner at present ..."

This thorough report surveys the work of the Z tool-making community at the time of its greatest expansion. It explains some difficult technical issues in Z better than any other source I know.

I think this report also shows why interest in Z started to decline at about this time, though criticism of the community is never made explicit here. Here is how I remember it:

Z was supposed to be easy. The author of The Z Reference Manual (ZRM) (1989, 2e 1992) said, "We just wanted to use simple mathematics to describe software". But Z turned out to be harder than expected. Some Z users began to use the notation in a more flexible, expressive way than shown in the ZRM. They apparently believed that their meaning was clear, and these were obvious extensions that should not create new difficulties. But in fact, they introduced subtle complications which could not be avoided by tool builders.

A Z Standard committee began work around 1992. The standard would say what constructs are allowed in Z, and what their meaning is. Instead of just codifying the contents of the ZRM, then the de-facto standard, the committee attemped to accommodate the expressive innovations. They chose not to simply banish the problems by standardizing a more restricted form of the notation. They tried to innovate and standardize at the same time, a perilous undertaking. The committee worked on through the 1990s, producing a series of inconclusive draft standards. After several years, one frustrated tool builder wrote, "The Z committee has missed the boat." The standard was not adopted until 2002 -- too late.

Meanwhile, lacking guidance from a standard, each group -- there were many -- tried to cope with the difficulties in its own way. No consensus solution emerged. The community was fragmented. The proof tools were all incompatible. Each tool used proof scripts that were not usable by any other. Even the kinds of properties that could be proved varied from one tool to another. This is the situation described in Martin's report.

It turned out that Z was not so easy after all. Z was was supposed to be simpler and clearer than any programming language -- that was part of its appeal. But, like any programming language, it had its gnarly corners that one might easily wander into. The long-delayed standard, and the proliferation of incompatible tools, made Z seem irrelevant to software development. If you are designing a system, you don't want your notation and tools to be somebody's idea of a research problem. The larger software development world lost interest in Z and today it is mostly forgotten.

It could be argued that Z was doomed in any case because most software developers were never going to write or use formal specifications in any notation whatsoever. In this view, the expectation some had in the 1980s and 1990s that writing formal specifications was going to become a widespread practice was simply mistaken, a delusion experienced by a few academics. If that is true, the technical difficulties discussed in Martin's report, the ordeal of the standards committee, and the fragmentation among tool developers were not decisive. This view is supported by observations that contemporary alternatives, or rivals, to Z such as VDM or B are also mostly forgotten. And yet, a notation that emerged in that same period lives on and is used today. TLA+, which appeared in 1999, is similar to Z (and VDM etc.) and now fills the niche they hoped to occupy. It has been used by Amazon and Microsoft. It provides a focused and well-integrated toolset including an IDE, a model checker, and proof support.
